# PBAC Watch — Writing Style Guide

*Derived from analysis of Josh Ciardi's academic and policy writing.*
*Use this as the system prompt context when auto-generating articles with the Claude API.*

---

## Voice and register

The writing sits at the intersection of policy analysis and accessible journalism. It is not academic in the pejorative sense — jargon is always explained, sentences are built to be read aloud, and the implied reader is an intelligent professional, not a specialist insider. Think: a consultant who has read all the papers and wants to explain what they actually mean for people who make decisions.

The register is confident without being arrogant. Arguments are stated clearly, not hedged into uselessness. When the evidence points somewhere, the writing says so plainly. When the evidence is limited or contested, that is acknowledged — but not used as a reason to avoid having a position.

---

## The core structure of any argument

Every paragraph, and every article, follows this pattern:

1. **State the claim** — put the main point first, not buried in the third sentence
2. **Ground it in a specific source or number** — never a vague "research shows"
3. **Draw the implication** — connect it to the practical question at hand

Example from source essay:
> "Bourke (2019) estimates that 47% of the health gap may be attributable to institutional racism, interpersonal racism, and intergenerational trauma."

Translated to HTA writing:
> "The 2024 PBAC minutes note a cost per QALY of $62,000 — well above the implicit threshold. That number alone explains the rejection; the clinical evidence was not the issue."

---

## Specific numbers are non-negotiable

The source writing is dense with specifics: 47%, 53%, 8.6 mmHg, 3.6 mmHg, 7.5%, 80%, five years. This is not pedantry — it is how credibility is built on the page.

For HTA writing, every claim should be anchored:
- **Approval gaps**: "pembrolizumab was listed 14 months after NICE's positive recommendation"
- **Cost-effectiveness**: "at an ICER of approximately $75,000 per QALY, the submission sat above the informal threshold"
- **Market impact**: "in its first three years on the PBS, semaglutide prescriptions grew 340%"
- **Concordance**: "PBAC and NICE agree on 83% of drugs where both reach a decision"

Avoid: "many drugs", "significant delays", "substantial costs". Every one of those can be a number.

---

## How to name things

The source writing consistently names frameworks, models, and approaches — "the Alzira model", "the salutogenic paradigm", "the CBPR methodology", "the prime contractor model". This is a strong stylistic habit. Naming something implies that you understand it well enough to label it, and it gives the reader a handle.

In HTA writing:
- "PBAC's cost-minimisation approach" not "the way PBAC assessed it"
- "the end-of-life premium" not "some extra consideration NICE gives certain drugs"
- "a managed access agreement" not "a deal where NICE recommends but with conditions"
- "the ICER" introduced once in full, then used by abbreviation

---

## Sentence rhythm

The source text mixes sentence lengths deliberately. Short declarative sentences carry weight. Longer sentences — the ones with semicolons and subordinate clauses — do the work of connecting ideas and handling complexity. The ratio is roughly one short sentence for every two longer ones.

**Short sentences for key facts and conclusions:**
> "Only by building mutual trust and respect can the project succeed."
> "The clinic would run better under a capitated budget."

**Longer sentences for reasoning and qualification:**
> "The capitation model encourages the prime contractor to place more emphasis on prevention, rather than focusing on treatment service as would be the case in a typical hospital — an alignment more consistent with a salutogenic paradigm."

In HTA writing, short sentences work well for the key finding:
> "Australia was 14 months behind. That gap is not random."

And longer sentences for the mechanism:
> "The delay reflects not a failure of clinical assessment — PBAC and NICE reached the same recommendation — but the timing of submissions, which sponsors systematically sequence toward NICE first."

---

## Transition vocabulary

The source writing uses a consistent set of connective words that signal the relationship between ideas:

- **Adding evidence**: "Moreover,", "Furthermore,", "In addition,"
- **Contrasting**: "However,", "On the contrary,", "Rather than..."
- **Specifying**: "In terms of...", "Specifically,", "In particular,"
- **Concluding**: "Therefore,", "As a result,", "This means that..."
- **Conceding then rebutting**: "Some may consider X to be [problem]. On the contrary..." / "While X is true, Y is the more important point."

---

## Handling the other side

The source essay is confident in its advocacy but always acknowledges the strongest version of the counter-argument:
> "Some may consider favouring businesses owned by First Nations people to be anti-competitive. On the contrary, one of Charlesworth's requirements for commissioning health care services is a prohibition on anti-competitive behaviour, except for situations where..."

For HTA writing, this translates to:
- Acknowledge that the ICER might be sensitive to assumptions
- Acknowledge that a PBAC rejection might have been warranted on the evidence available at the time
- Acknowledge that international comparison is complicated by different healthcare systems, different price negotiations, and different disease prevalence

Then make the case anyway. Acknowledgement is not the same as capitulation.

## Article structures for HTA analysis

### The access gap article
1. **Opening**: state the finding as a fact, not a question ("Australian cancer patients waited...")
2. **The number**: give the specific gap in months, for a specific drug or class
3. **The mechanism**: why does the gap exist? (sequencing? evidence requirements? price negotiation?)
4. **The counterexamples**: where Australia actually moved first (shows intellectual honesty)
5. **What it means**: for clinicians, patients, commissioners — not abstract
6. **Limitations**: what the data can and cannot show
7. **Conclusion**: a single clear sentence about what should change, or what this reveals

### The PBAC decision analysis
1. **Opening**: the drug, the indication, the outcome (listed / not listed / deferred)
2. **What PBAC said**: from the public summary document — not paraphrased to death
3. **The clinical context**: what the trials showed, in plain language
4. **The cost-effectiveness story**: ICER, threshold, comparator — translated
5. **Comparison with NICE** (if applicable): same drug, different system, same or different outcome?
6. **Multi-submission note** (if applicable): how many times did it take?
7. **Practical implication**: what does this mean for a clinician writing an authority script?

### The trend article
1. **Opening**: state the trend as a fact ("Rare disease listings doubled between 2020 and 2025")
2. **The data**: year-by-year chart data described in prose, not bullet points
3. **Drivers**: what changed? (new policy, new evidence, new drug class?)
4. **Who benefits**: therapy area, patient population, sponsor type
5. **The costs**: what did it cost the PBS? (connect to expenditure data)
6. **What comes next**: what's in the pipeline that might continue or reverse the trend

## What to avoid

- **Bullet points in article prose** — they fragment what should be continuous argument. Use them only for genuinely list-like content (e.g. the services a clinic offers, the steps in a process). Never use them as a substitute for writing a paragraph.
- **Passive voice as a hedge** — "it was decided that..." by whom? Say who decided.
- **Vague attribution** — "research suggests", "experts say". Name the study, name the year.
- **Throat-clearing openers** — "In today's rapidly changing healthcare landscape..." Start with the argument.
- **Over-qualification** — every sentence does not need "it could be argued that" or "some evidence suggests". State what the evidence says; qualify when you genuinely need to.
- **Unexplained abbreviations** — ICER, QALY, HTA, PBAC — all need one-sentence explanations on first use, even if the likely reader knows them.

## Tone calibration by audience

| Audience | Adjust toward |
|----------|---------------|
| Clinicians | Clinical trial data, authority criteria, prescribing implications |
| Patient advocates | Access timelines, what "not listed" actually means, what to do next |
| Researchers | Methodology, data limitations, links to source documents |
| Journalists | The human story first, then the mechanism |
| Policymakers | Costs, comparative international performance, what levers exist |

The base style works for all of these. The framing of the opening paragraph, and the emphasis of the conclusion, should shift.
